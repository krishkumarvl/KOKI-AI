DESIGNED WITH INTENTION

Every design decision has a reason.

KOKI wasn't designed to look futuristic. It was designed to feel calm, human and welcoming — and it wasn't built with one app, or on the first try. Here's the actual process, not the polished version.

The First Attempt (And Why It Got Scrapped)

The very first version of this website wasn't parchment and green at all — it was a full 90s retro theme. Chunky pixels, nostalgic colours, the whole vibe. I loved it while I was building it.

But I asked myself one honest question: would I still want to use this in 15 minutes, or am I just excited right now? The answer was no. A retro theme is fun to look at once, not something you want to sit inside every day while actually building a product. So it got scrapped — not deleted from my head, though. Some of that nostalgic energy will show up somewhere in KOKI later, just not as the entire identity of the site.

Where the Inspiration Came From

Once the retro idea was gone, I went looking for what actually felt right, and a few things shaped the current look directly:

  • Claude's interface — clean, minimal, almost no visual noise. It taught me that a product can feel premium just by getting out of the user's way.
  • GitHub's contribution graph — the little "how many days you were active" pattern. That's where the idea of tracking and showing KOKI's build streak openly came from.
  • Pinterest — used it to study what kinds of website design actually exist, which colour palette combinations feel good together, and to find a visual "base" to build from instead of guessing blind.
  • Studying how established designers think — not to copy layouts, but to understand their approach and broaden my own perspective on what good design decisions look like.

None of it was copied directly. The palette, the type pairing, the atelier framing — that part is mine. The research just gave me a wider set of references to choose from instead of designing in a vacuum.

The Boy Who Wanted to Build

There is something I've never really grown out of. And honestly... I don't want to.

When I was a kid, I wasn't dreaming about becoming famous. I wasn't thinking about money. I wasn't even thinking about starting a company. I was just fascinated by one simple idea. Building things.

Movies became more than entertainment for me. They became windows into worlds where impossible ideas became real. Every time I watched someone create something extraordinary, I wasn't thinking, "That's cool." I was thinking, "How did they build that?"

Looking back now... I think that's where everything actually started. Not KOKI. Not programming. Not AI. Just curiosity.

The kind of curiosity that keeps asking questions even after everyone else has stopped. The kind that opens another browser tab at two in the morning. The kind that turns "What if?" into "Let's find out."

Curiosity was my first superpower.

I didn't know it then. But that small feeling would quietly shape almost every decision I'd make later.

Iron Man

People often ask me why I like Iron Man so much. Most of them assume it's because of the suits. And honestly... the suits are still one of the coolest things I've ever seen. Even today, I get excited whenever I see a new one.

But that's never been the real reason. If all I cared about was the armour, I probably would've forgotten about him years ago. What stayed with me wasn't the technology. It was Tony Stark himself.

He looked at problems differently. While everyone else saw something impossible... he saw something waiting to be solved. Every challenge looked like another experiment. Another opportunity to build. Another reason to learn something new.

One scene has stayed with me ever since I first watched it. People remember it because of one famous line.

"Tony Stark built this in a cave."

Whenever I hear that line... I don't think about the Arc Reactor. I don't think about the suit. I think about something much bigger.

Imagine losing everything. Your resources. Your comfort. Your freedom. Almost your life. And still choosing to build instead of giving up. That mindset changed the way I look at problems.

Life will never be perfect. There will always be something missing. More money. More knowledge. Better equipment. More experience. But if I keep waiting for perfect conditions... I'll never build anything.

Tony Stark taught me that you don't need perfect conditions. You need curiosity. You need resilience. And you need the courage to start with whatever you have.

Don't wait for the perfect workshop. Start building with the tools you already have.

I think that's why I enjoy creating so much. Not because I know everything. But because every project teaches me something I didn't know yesterday. Every bug. Every redesign. Every late night. Every failure. They're all part of becoming a better builder.

Maybe one day I'll build something incredible. Maybe I won't. But I never want to lose that mindset. The belief that no matter how difficult life becomes... there is always something you can build next.

Spider-Man

If Iron Man made me fall in love with building... Spider-Man taught me the kind of person I want to become.

Out of every superhero I've ever watched... Peter Parker has always been my favourite. Not because he's the strongest. Not because he always wins. Not because he has the coolest powers. Actually... it's almost the opposite.

He's just a normal person. A student trying to balance life, responsibilities, dreams and the people he loves. Sometimes life gives him more pain than happiness. Sometimes it feels like everything is taken away from him. And yet... he still gets up the next morning.

He never lets pain become an excuse to stop being kind.

That's what I admire. Not the web shooters. Not the suit. Not even the powers. His character.

He loses people he loves. He struggles with money. He makes mistakes. He questions himself. He feels alone. He carries responsibilities that nobody else understands. Honestly... I think that's why I relate to him so much.

Life isn't always fair. Sometimes you work hard and still fail. Sometimes people misunderstand you. Sometimes you lose things you never wanted to lose. But Spider-Man always reminds me of something simple.

No matter how heavy life becomes... don't lose your smile.

One thing I love about Peter Parker is that he never lets difficult moments change who he is. He still jokes. He still laughs. He still helps people. Not because life is easy. But because that's the person he chooses to be.

That inspires me far more than any superpower ever could. Anyone can be strong when everything is going well. Real strength is staying kind when life gives you every reason not to.

If Iron Man inspired the builder inside me... Spider-Man reminds me to protect the human inside me. To stay humble. To take responsibility. To keep moving forward. And maybe... to smile a little more often.

Because at the end of the day... I don't just want to build great technology. I want to become someone whose presence makes people feel a little lighter. Someone who brings warmth into a room. Someone who leaves people better than they found them. I think that's a much bigger achievement than building software.

"What's the Point?"

It's probably the question I've heard the most. Sometimes people ask it directly. Sometimes they don't say it out loud, but I can see it in their eyes.

"Why build another AI?"

There are already hundreds of AI assistants. Some are smarter. Some are faster. Some have teams of thousands of engineers behind them. Some have billions of dollars. Compared to them... KOKI is tiny.

So why build it? Why spend nights debugging? Why learn things outside college? Why stay awake until four in the morning trying to fix one small problem? Why keep building something that might never become the biggest?

Honestly... I don't have a complicated answer.

Because I want to.

That's it.

I build because I'm curious. I build because creating something from nothing makes me happy. I build because every project teaches me something I didn't know yesterday.

I'm not trying to beat anyone. I'm not trying to prove that KOKI is better than every AI assistant out there. That was never the goal.

There are incredible people building incredible things. I admire them. I've learned from many of them. KOKI doesn't exist to replace their work. It exists because I wanted to experience the joy of building something with my own hands.

I don't build to compete. I build because creating makes me feel alive.

Maybe only one person will ever use KOKI. Maybe millions will. Honestly... I don't know.

But if even one person finds an idea, a feature, a sentence or a moment that genuinely helps them... then every late night, every bug and every redesign will have been worth it.

Not everything needs to become the biggest thing in the world to become meaningful. Sometimes... the act of creating is already enough.

And maybe that's the real point. Not to build the biggest AI. Not to build the most famous product. But to build something that reminds me why I fell in love with technology in the first place.

Curiosity

People often tell me, "You should focus on one thing." And honestly... I understand why they say that. The world rewards specialists. It rewards people who become incredibly good at one thing. There's nothing wrong with that.

But I've never wanted to experience life that way.

I don't want to know everything. I just don't want to stop discovering.

I'm curious about almost everything. Technology. Music. Movies. Art. History. Philosophy. Business. Psychology. Travel. Space. Design. Programming. Content creation. Poetry. Photography. Guitar. Sometimes I learn something simply because one random question appeared in my mind.

People ask me, "How are all these things connected?" To me... they already are.

Movies teach me how to tell stories. Music teaches me how emotions can exist without words. Programming teaches me patience. Design teaches me that small details change everything. Business teaches responsibility. History teaches perspective. Travel reminds me who I am when life becomes too noisy. Every subject quietly teaches something the others cannot.

That's why I don't believe curiosity has a syllabus. Learning doesn't have to stay inside a classroom. Sometimes the most valuable lesson comes from a movie. Sometimes from a conversation with a stranger. Sometimes from getting completely lost in a new city.

Perfection is just a destination. Curiosity is a way of living.

Maybe I'll never become the best programmer. Maybe I'll never become the best designer. Maybe I'll never become the best entrepreneur. And honestly... I'm okay with that.

Because my goal has never been to become the best at one thing. My goal is to experience as much of life as I possibly can. To keep learning. To keep creating. To keep wondering.

One day we'll all leave this world behind. So before that day comes... I want to make sure I actually lived. Not just existed.

Maybe that's why I keep learning random things. Not because I have to. Because I genuinely enjoy becoming a little different from who I was yesterday.

If Money Was Never the Problem

Sometimes people ask me, "What would you do if money was never a problem?" It's an interesting question. Because the answer says a lot about who you are.

For me... the first thing wouldn't be another company. It wouldn't be another AI. It wouldn't even be technology.

I'd build peace for my family first.

The people who believed in me long before I had anything to show. The people who sacrificed quietly while I kept dreaming loudly. They deserve a life where they never have to worry again. That would always come first.

After that... I'd build schools. But probably not the kind of schools we already have.

I don't want classrooms where curiosity slowly disappears because students become afraid of making mistakes. I want classrooms where asking questions is celebrated. Where creativity isn't treated like a distraction. Where children don't feel guilty for dreaming differently.

I want a place where programming sits beside poetry. Where philosophy is as important as physics. Where music is respected just as much as mathematics. Where children learn how money works... how kindness works... how technology works... and most importantly... how to think for themselves.

Education shouldn't create perfect workers. It should help people discover who they really are.

I'd build hospitals. I'd invest in research. I'd support people with ideas who only needed someone to believe in them. I'd help wherever I genuinely could. Not because I wanted to be remembered. Because if you have the ability to make someone's life a little easier... why wouldn't you?

And yes... I'd travel. A lot. Not because I want expensive vacations. Because travelling reminds me who I am. Whenever life becomes too loud... whenever everything starts feeling repetitive... the road has a strange way of making me feel alive again.

Sometimes I travel to discover new places. Sometimes I travel to rediscover myself.

Maybe none of these dreams happen exactly the way I imagine them today. That's okay. Dreams evolve. People evolve. Life evolves.

But I hope one thing never changes. I hope I never stop using whatever I have to leave the world a little kinder than I found it.

What KOKI Really Means

People often see KOKI as another AI project. A chatbot. An assistant. A piece of software. And honestly... I understand why. That's what it looks like from the outside.

But to me... KOKI has never just been about artificial intelligence.

KOKI is a collection of my curiosity.

Every feature has a story. Every redesign reminds me of a mistake I learned from. Every bug reminds me of a night I refused to give up. Every line of code carries a memory. Sometimes excitement. Sometimes frustration. Sometimes both at the same time.

When I look at KOKI... I don't only see software. I see every version of myself that helped build it. The beginner who didn't know where to start. The student who kept learning after making mistakes. The developer who kept rewriting things because they could be better. The dreamer who believed an idea was worth chasing, even when nobody was asking for it.

KOKI isn't just growing. I'm growing with it.

People sometimes ask me, "If someone offered you millions to buy KOKI... would you sell it?" Honestly... I don't think I could. Not because I believe it's priceless. But because some things carry value that money simply can't measure.

KOKI has become part of my journey. It's where I've poured my ideas, my failures, my late nights, my excitement and my curiosity. Selling it would feel like giving away a chapter of my own life.

Maybe one day KOKI becomes something much bigger than I ever imagined. Maybe it stays a small project that only a handful of people know about. I genuinely don't know.

But I do know this. Whether KOKI succeeds or fails... it has already given me something I'll carry forever. Confidence. Patience. Discipline. Curiosity. And the courage to build ideas instead of only dreaming about them.

People think I built KOKI. The truth is... KOKI has been quietly building me too.

Maybe that's why this project means so much to me. Because every new version of KOKI is also a new version of myself. And that's something I never want to stop creating.

One Day...

Sometimes I imagine myself many years from now. Older. Hopefully a little wiser. Maybe sitting somewhere in the mountains where everything is quiet except the wind. Watching the sunrise with a cup of tea in my hands.

I imagine someone sitting beside me and asking one simple question.

"Was building KOKI worth it?"

I don't think I'd answer immediately. I'd probably smile first. Because that question isn't really about KOKI. It's about the life I chose to live.

Maybe KOKI became successful. Maybe it didn't. Maybe millions of people used it. Maybe only a handful ever discovered it. Honestly... I don't know.

But I know something much more important.

I'd rather spend my life building things that might fail... than spend my life wondering, "What if I had tried?"

Even if KOKI never becomes the biggest AI in the world... I'll still be proud of it. Because I tried. Because I learned. Because I experienced something that no book, no course and no tutorial could ever teach me.

KOKI gave me late nights. Moments of frustration. Moments of excitement. Moments where one tiny bug made me question everything. And moments where one tiny breakthrough reminded me exactly why I started.

Maybe that's what success really is. Not reaching the destination. But becoming someone better because you chose to begin the journey.

If someone remembers me one day... I don't want it to be because I built an AI. I hope they remember me as someone who stayed kind. Someone who stayed curious. Someone who wasn't afraid to dream out loud. Someone whose presence made people feel a little lighter.

And if one person reading this decides to finally start building the idea they've been carrying for years... Then KOKI has already become something meaningful.

Maybe the greatest thing I ever build won't be software. Maybe it'll be the courage to inspire someone else to build.

That's a legacy I'd be proud of.
